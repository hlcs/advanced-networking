Getting Connected

To use a network, you must first choose a way to connect to it. Wirings and wireless are popular choices.

Thanks to Integrated Service Routers (ISR) and their multiple ports, switching, and Wireless Access Point, you have increasing flexibility.

Purpose of Physical Layer

Physical Layer takes the frame resulting from all of the above layers and encodes the bit into a series of signals for transmitting.

The destination physical layer decodes the signal and restore the bit representing the frame, and passes it to upper layers.

Common Media for Physical Layer

  • Copper: represents bits through electricity.
  • Fiber-optic: represents bits through light patterns.
  • Wireless: represents bits through radio waves.

L1 Fundamentals: Physical Components

Every physical layer involves by definition at least some physical components involved in generating and trasmitting the signals, such as cables, connectors, network interfaces, ports.

L1 Fundamentals: Signaling

The signaling method is the method of representing bits.

Common signaling methods in networking are changes in electromagnetics fields, voltage variations, or waves modulation.

Signals can be asynchronous or synchronous. The difference is in the presence of an associated clock signal.

L1 Fundamentals: Encoding

Bits need to be sent in a specific pattern that is recognizable at both ends. This is called a code.

Common codes are the Manchester Encoding (bits are represented by transitions) and the Non-Return to Zero (bits are represented by specific ranges).

Bandwidth and Throughput

Data transfer speed usually is discussed in terms of

  • Bandwidth: (maximum) theoretical capacity of a medium to carry data.

  • Throughput: it's the actual measure of how many bits flow across the media in a perioid of time.

They are measured in bps (bit per seconds) and multiples.

A lot of factor contributes to throughput not being able to reach theoretical bandwidth: physics, media quality, amount and type of traffic, latency.

Copper Cabling: Characteristics

Copper media is great for networking because it's cheap, easy to deploy and has low resistance to current. But...

... it also suffers from signal attenuation over long distances, so you have a maximum distance to respect.

Copper cabling can suffer from 2 kinds of interferences:

  • Electromagnetic (EMI) or radiofrequency (RFI) interferences from other sources
  • Crosstalk from one wire to an adjacent one.

There are countermeasures: metallic shielding, cable twisting, cable twisting disparity.

Copper Cabling: UTP

Unshielded twisted-pair (UTP) is the most common network media. They are terminated by RJ45 connectors.

They are composed by:

  • An outer jacket the protects the wire.
  • Twisted pairs of wires to transmit the signal.
  • Color-Coded insulation.

Copper Cabling: STP

They cost more, and are more difficult to install, but shielded twisted-pair cable provide better interference protection.

They combine shielding and crosstalk protection with special connectors (that can make the cable act like an antenna if unproperly grounded)

Shielding (a metallic foil) on STP cabling can be applied:

  • On the wire bundle
  • On the wire bundle and on each wire separately

Copper Cabling: Coaxial

In coaxial cables there are only two concentric conductors, separated by plastic insulation and surrounded by a foil shielding.

Coaxial cables have many type of connectors, among the popular choices are BNC, N-type, F-type, RP-SMA

Nowadays in networking they are mostly used for attaching antenna to wireless devices.

Copper Cabling: Safety

Copper cabling hasn't a very good resistance to ignition.

The main thing you have to do with copper cabling, aside from assuring integrity, is to correctly ground them.

UTP Cabling Standards

UTP Cabling Standards

The TIA/EIA-568 standards specifies cable types, lengths, connector, pinouts, cable termination and testing method for LAN cable installations.

RJ45 is the 8P8C physical connector used by the Ethernet standard (and many others). Proper termination of cables at the RJ45 connectors is extremely important.

UTP cables are ranked by IEEE into categories that are performance-based. Cat 5e is the most common LAN Category.

  • Cat. 3, for voice/phone lines
  • Cat. 5 and 5e, up to 100 Mb/s and 1 Gb/s respectively
  • Cat. 6 and 6a, up to 10 Gb/s

UTP Wiring Conventions

The pinouts (wiring order and corresponding pins) of UTP cables varies according to the use case.

  • Ethernet Straight-through: same convention at both ends of the cable, it is the most common type of cable for networking and it's used to connect an host to a switch, or a switch to a router
  • Ethernet Crossover: different conventions at each end of the cable, it's used for connecting two similar devices, like two hosts, two switches, two routers.
  • Rollover: Cisco proprietary for console port usage.

No damage can happen by using the wrong cable, but unless there is some auto-sensing mechanism, no communication will occur. It should be the first point of any troubleshooting.

UTP Cable Testing

For anything other than simple functional tests, such as

  • Wire map
  • Cable length
  • Signal attenuation
  • Crosstalk

A UTP Cable tester should be used.

Fiber Optic Cabling: Carachteristics

Optical fiber is a flexible and extremely thin transparent wire of pure glass. It acts as a guide for light impulses to which bits are encoded.

Fiber Optics allows for longer distances and higher data rates than any other media.

It is also immune to any electromagnetic interference.

It is used for:

  • Backbone applications
  • Broadband delivery (FTTN/B/H)
  • Long distances networks
  • Submarine networks

Fiber Optic Cabling: Design

Fiber optic cables have a simple but highly technological structure:

  • Core - the glass through which the light is transmitted.
  • Cladding - it is the glass that surrounds the core. It acts as a mirror, reflecting the light trying to escape the core, keeping it in.
  • Jacket - A stengthened PVC enclosure that protects the glass.

Fiber Optic Cabling: Types

The lights travelling through a fiber optic cable can be generated by lasers or by LEDs. Photodiodes will catch the light at the other end and convert it to data frames.

Fiber optics cables are mainly of two categories:

  • Single-Mode Fiber (SMF): very expensive, it uses laser and a very small core to produce a single ray that can travel very long distances with minimal losses. Yellow is the standard color.
  • Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF): LED emitting through a larger core, entering the fiber at different angles. Up to 500m at 10 Gb/s. Orange/Aqua is the standard color.

Fiber Optic Cabling: Connectors

Nowadays, the most common FO connectors are:

  • ST: bayonet-style for MMF.
  • SC: it is the "standard" connector, has a push-pull mechanism that keeps it in place. Can be use for MMF and SMF.
  • LC: smaller sized connector, used mainly for SMF but also supports MMF.

In FO cabling light travels only in one direction. So you need two bundled cables to be able to tx/rx simultaneously.

Unused fiber cables should be protected with a plastic cap.

You need patch cord to connect fiber infrastructure devices.

Fiber Optic Cabling: Testing

Terminating and cutting fiber-optic cables is definitely not as easy as with copper. It requires special equipment and skills.

Typical errors in FO termination and splicing:

  • Misalignment of two joined fibers.
  • End gap, the media does not touch at the connection point.
  • End finish, the media has not a precise termination and presents some dirt.

A quick test is to shine a light at one end and see if it comes to the other one: it doesn't tell you about performances, but it cheaply finds a broken fiber.

Wireless Standards

Wireless media that we commonly encounter includes:

  • IEEE 802.11 - Wireless LAN (WLAN), we call Wi-Fi
  • IEEE 802.15 - Wireless Personal Area Network (WPAN), commonly known as Bluetooth
  • IEEE 802.16 - WiMAX, uses a PtMP topology and was specifically developed to provide broadband access.

Wireless LAN

To connect wirelessly to a LAN you need:

  • a Wireless Access Point (WAP or AP) that signals the availability of a wifi connection and aggregates the signals, connecting user to an existing wired infrastructure.
  • a Wireless NIC adapter, providing wireless capabilities to individual hosts.

Data Link Layer

The Data Link layer performs two functions:

  • It takes L3 PDUs and generates data units called frames, that are ready for transmission over the media.
  • It controls media access, that is how data is placed on the media, and performs error detection.

In essence it hides the specifics of the media from every upper layer , which they don't need to know.

Data Link Sublayers: LLC and MAC

The Data Link Layer is divided in LLC and MAC sublayers.

The Logical Link Control (LLC) is the interface to the Network Layer in the stack, it identifies which L3 protocol is in use and de/multiplexes it into/from the frame.

The Media Access Control (MAC) is the interface to the hardware, it generates the frame, attaching data link addresses and delimitations, and then physically coordinates with the hardware to send it.

LLC is a software (the NIC driver), while MAC is implemented in hardware

Media Access Control

The media access control method is the technique used for placing the frame on and off the media.

Example of MAC methods are CSMA, TDMA, Polling, etc. They depend on the topology and whether the media is to be contented or not.

While L3 protocols may stay largely the same, we are bound to see many different data link and media transitions over a remote communication.

Routers de-encapsulate and re-encapsulate properly according to L1 and L2 requirements on that network segment.

Layer 2 Frame Structure

A L2 frame contains the PDU of L3 plus control informations, that is information that allows the protocol to carry on its functions.

A L2 frame is composed by:

  • An Header at the beginning, containing addressing information.
  • then Data, in the form of the L3 PDU.
  • and at the end, a Trailer containing other control infos like error detection.

A Generic L2 Frame

Structure of frame and fields contained in header and trailer vary according to the DLL protocol. There can't be a single one because needs of transportation across media types are very different.

Data Link Layer Standards

Because of the close relationship between L2 and L1, many Data Link standards actually defines both.

WAN Topologies: Point-to-Point

Permanent link between only 2 end points.

WAN Topologies: Hub and Spoke

A central node interconnecting many PtP links.

WAN Topologies: Mesh

Every node is PtP interconnected to every other node.

Physical vs Logical PtP

When two nodes are physically connected in a PtP topology, media sharing is not a concern, and so isn't addressing. DLL protocols can therefore be much more simpler.

When the two nodes that we want to connect haven't a direct physical connection, we must established a logical PtP connection though a virtual circuit.

In logical PtP, the MAC method and the DLL still operate as if the connection was physical.

Half and Full Duplex

In a PtP topology, two devices can communicate:

  • Half-Duplex: both can TX/RX, but not simultaneously. This mandates for conflict resolution about media usage.
  • Full-Duplex: both can TX/TX and can do that at the same time. This means the media is assumed uncontended by the DLL.

Physical LAN Topologies: Star

End devices connected to a central device, that used to be an hub but now is a switch. Most common LAN topology.

Physical LAN Topologies: Extended Star

Several star topologies are interconnected together through other star topologies (Extended Star) or through a bus (Hybrid)

Physical LAN Topologies: Bus

No intermediary device required, all end system chained toghether in some form or another.

Physical LAN Topologies: Ring

Each end device is connected to its neighbours, forming a ring.

Sharing media on LAN topologies

There are many MAC methods, but the two basic fall in these 2 categories:

  • Contention-based access: the medium is contested (hosts can send data when it has them), but nodes know what to do if a collision happens.
  • Controlled access: each node has its own allocated time for medium usage.

Carrier Sense Multiple Access (CSMA)

Used in contention-based medium access to detect if there's a signal (carrier) on the media before transmitting.

The more nodes there are the less probable is to be able to use the media without collisions.

Coupled with a mean for resolving collision:

  • CSMA/CD for Ethernet 802.3: if another signal is perceived while transmitting, all devices stop transmitting and try later.
  • CSMA/CA for 802.11: sends notification of intent to use media and wait for clearance.

Controlled/Scheduled Access

Devices access the medium one after the other, and then have to wait for their turn again.

A device must first acquire the token to be able to so. When it has one and wants to transmit, it fills the token with data and send it.

No other device can transmit until the frame has arrived and the token got released.

Examples: Token Ring (IEEE 802.5) and Fiber Distributed Data Interface (FDDI).

Ring Topology

It is a multi-access topology, because every frame is received by each node (on the path between source and destination), but addressed only by the intended destination.

If a node receives a message that is not destined to it, it relays it to the next node.

Using a token is the most common MAC method for ring topologies.
