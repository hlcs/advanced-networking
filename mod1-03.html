What is Communication?

What are the elements of a communication?

  • The message, of course.
  • The source of the message, the sender
  • The destination of the message, the receiver
  • The channel in which communication occurs.
  • The rules, or protocols to which both sender and receiver have to agree.

These elements belongs to every kind of communication.

Rules of a communication

By following established protocols that governs communications, it is possible to deliver and understand a message reliably.

There can be many protocols. Each one provides an agreement on the following requirements:

  • Identifying sender and receiver
  • Commong language and grammar structures
  • Speed and timing
  • Confirmation or acknowledgment of delivery

What Protocols do

Message Encoding

Encoding is converting information between two different forms.

Decoding is the reverse process.

Examples: words -> sounds; bits -> impulses

What Protocols do

Message Formatting and Encapsulation

Now that we have an encoded message, we must send it with appropriate structure

Letters' recipient, greeting, content, closing, signature are perfect examples.

Most of the times is convenient to enclose the message for delivery, like we do with envelopes for letters.

Enclosing one message format in another is called Encapsulation. De-encapsulation is the reverse process.

Computers' envelopes are called frames.

What Protocols do

Message Size

Messages are usually divided into smaller parts, suited for how much the receiver can process at one time.

This division process is called segmenting.

Each segment must abide to both a minimum and maximum size.

What Protocols do

Message Timing

  • Access Method: when can I speak? what do I do if I speak when others are speaking too?
  • Flow Control: how fast/slow and for how long can I speak?
  • Response Timeout: how long do I wait for a response before reacting? what is the appropriate reaction when the other party is not responding?

What Protocols do

Message Delivery Options

  • Unicast: one-to-one delivery.
  • Multicast: one-to-many delivery.
  • Broadcast: one-to-all delivery.

Sometimes the receiver is required to send confirmation (acknowledgment or ack) that it actually received the message.

Protocols in Stacks: Theory

Protocol Suite: several protocols that needs to interact toghether for a communication to occour.

This interactions is best thought (model) in terms of layers in a stack.

Each upper layer protocols depends on the functionality provided by lower level protocols.

Rule of thumb: lower layer protocols are more concerned with moving data, upper layer ones with message content.

Protocols in Stacks: Practice

We can see the protocol stack model in action even in a simple communication between a web server and a browser.

  • Application Protocol: HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol), requesting the right page to the web server.
  • Transport Protocol: TCP (Transmission Control Protocol), it helps in splitting a single big page into multiple pieces that can be reassambled.
  • Internet Protocol: IP, it identifies source and destination by address.
  • Network Access Protocol: eg. Ethernet, placing the bits on to the wires.

Protocol Suites

Protocol suites can be developed by a standards organization or by a vendor.

The TCP/IP Protocol Suite (IP, HTTP, DHCP and others) is a standard-based protocol suite. This means they are freely available to the public for implementation.

Following standards-based protocols allows for interoperability. Hw and Sw from different vendors are guaranteed to work together.

Some protocols are proprietary: only one vendor develops, controls and (often) knows how the protocol operates.

Open Standards Organizations

Standards are essentials for an open Internet, and they wouldn't happen without vendor-neutral, non-profit standard organizations.

By producing freely available protocol specifications they ensure that no single vendor can hold the Internet hostage of their own interests.

Very often the standards these organizations produce are guaranteed to be royalty-free, which means no payment is due for the patents used in them.

The Internet Society

ISOC is an ensemble mainly focused on the development of the technical structure of the Internet.

This organization is run by a board of 13 respected individuals, and involves people not tied to any specific company, agency, etc.

The IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) maintain Internet technologies in the form of RFCs (Requests for Comments). The IRTF (Internet Research Task Force) is more focused on forward-thinking research to explore future directions.

ICANN and IANA

The Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA) is an organizations originally run by the US government for managing

  • IP address allocation,
  • domain names
  • protocol identifiers
  • port numbers

and Internet namespaces in general at the higher level.

The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN) is the non-profit that, over time, has carried over IANA duties under a contract with the US Government.

IEEE

The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (pronounced I-triple-E) is dedicated to standards and innovation in electrical engineering and electronics.

Its standards affects whole industries besides just networking.

Standard are grouped in families, which are referred to by a number, followed by a dot and another number identifying a more specific area and its working group.

IEEE 802 family of standard deals with LANs and MANs. Particularly interesting to us are 802.3 (Ethernet) and 802.11 (Wireless LAN) standards.

ISO

The Internation Organization for Standardization is the largest developer of standards, covering almost every possible field, products and services.

ISO is best known in networking for developing the OSI, Open System Interconnection reference model.

Its purposes was to produce a layered framework for networking protocols but also applying in a full protocol suite for the Internet. But TCP/IP already won...

ISO 9660 should ring a bell if you ever burned a CD.

EIA, TIA, ITU-T

The EIA, Electonic Induestries Alliance, is best known for standardizing wiring and connectors and mounting racks.

The TIA, Telecommunications Industry Association, collaborates with the EIA and produces standards for radio, cellular and satellite equipment, and also VoIP devices.

The International Telecommunications Union-Telecommunication Standardization Sector (ITU-T) is responsible for country codes for international dialing and broadband communication standards.

Layered Model

A layered model for protocols helps to visualize what are their operation and the interactions between them.

A protocol model describes how an actual protocol suite operates. A reference model describes no particular suite, focusing on the highest possible level of abstraction.

OSI Reference Model

ISO's intention was to develop a framework for protocol suites, and also recommending a protocol suite for Internetworking. It was half a success, TCP/IP taking the second half.

OSI 7 Layers Model, defining functions, domains and interactions between each layer and the ones directly below and above, has helped developed all types of protocols and networks.

Its 7 Layers are: Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application.

OSI Reference Model

L1: Physical

The Physical Layer deals with the hardware: it describes the mechanics of the actual, practical, activation and maintainance of a physical connection for transmitting and receiving digital bits.

OSI Reference Model

L2: Data Link

Once you are can trasmit your bits from two points on a common media, they can't be random bits. They need to have a structure, they need to access the channel reliably. Data link protocols deals with producing the bits into frames for transmission over the physical media.

OSI Reference Model

L3: Network

The Network Layer describes protocols to identify the end devices that needs to communicate and making individual packets of data travelling from one to the other(s).

OSI Reference Model

L4: Transport

The transport layer protocols define how to segment the data belonging to each communication, transfer them and then reassembling them at the destination end device.

OSI Reference Model

L5: Session

The first four layers can successfully deliver data from one end device to another, but it's this layer that allows two applications to coordinate their dialogue and data exchange.

Session layer deals with authentication, authorization and also session restoration.

OSI Reference Model

L6: Presentation

Applications want to transfer their data, but that raises the problem of having established and common way to represent those data. Session layer takes care of this.

OSI Reference Model

L7: Application

The Application is the interface between people and the transmission of data over a network. It initiates the process of running through the stack of protocols on behalf of the user.

TCP/IP Protocol Model

TCP/IP only defines 4 layers: Network Access, Internet, Transport and Application.

It closely match OSI model in Network and Transport layers, but it aggregates many functions in Network Access and Application layers.

Why Segmenting Data?

One long, continuous stream of bits would mean:

  • Busy channels for a long time
  • Full retransmission in case of errors

Segmenting data into smaller pieces makes them more managable.

By segmenting you then need multiplexing to interleave different communication's segments over a channel.

Protocol Data Units (PDU)

During the encapsulation process, application data passing through each layer gets added information by each protocol.

After protocols add information to the data, the data as a whole will assume a form closely related to the protocol, called PDU (Protocol Data Unit).

PDU at each layer have a different name to reflect their functions:

  • Data - General term for Application Layer
  • Segment - PDU of the Transport Layer
  • Packet - PDU of the Network Layer
  • Frame - PDU of the Data Link Layer
  • Bits - Physical Layer PDU

Addresses

Network and Data Link Layer are those responsible for uniquely identifying the source and destination of the data exchange.

They both do that using addresses, but of different kind and for different purposes.

Network Address: IP Addresses

Network addresses are logical addresses (meaning they are not tied to a specific device) required to deliver a packet when the 2 end devices are on different networks.

IP addresses consist of two parts, a network part and an host part.

  • The network part is used by router to identify and reach the destination network.
  • The host part is used by the last router to reach the destination host.

You need two of those IP address: source and destination.

Data Link Address: MAC Addresses

Data Link addresses, also known as physical addresses (because they are tied to the NIC), are used to reach another network interface in the same network.

Data Link addresses, like MAC addresses, are required because you can't really skip communicating in the same network:

  • Either you're trying to actually communicating with a device on the same network
  • or even if you're trying to reach another network, you need to reach at least another device of your own network to get to it

MAC addresses are the 48-bit (eg: C0:4A:00:34:50:91) long Data Link addresses for Ethernet networks. You need two: source and destination.

How do I get to know these addresses?

A host needs to know both the logical and physical addresses of the destination host to be able to generate and send the frame.

For IP addresses, you probably know IP addresses on your own network and you enter them manually.

For remote networks, you can learn IP address by configuring you r device to use DNS (Domain Name System).

Remembering MAC Addresses would be much more trickier, but fortunally ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) relieves you of that need.

Default Gateway

The default gateway is the router to which a host on a network will send every packet that it doesn't already know where to forward to.

The default gateway is presumed to know what to do.

Default gateway either routes the packet to final destination or to another router, called next hop.

If default gateway is misconfigured or missing, hosts cannot deliver packets to remote networks.
