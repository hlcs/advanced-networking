Why Wireless

Wireless technologies have several benefits that can be stated in a variety of forms but can all be reduced into a single one: wireless is a big comfort because it enables mobility.

This fact is reflected in overall easyness of use, configuration, maintenance, increase in productivity for the same cost / achieving the same results with a decrease in costs.

Wireless has particular needs for a correct deployment and is even more challenging to properly secure. We will see both these aspects in this chapter.

Types of Wireless

WPAN (Wireless Personal Area Network)

Technologies that communicate wirelessly in the direct surrounding of a person.

  • Bluetooth (IEEE 802.15) allows direct communication between paired devices, up to 10m with ordinary power consumption (which is particularly low, making it a good choice for mobile devices).
  • Several revisions (latest is the low-energy, 24 Mb/s 4.x).
  • Bluetooth is a general-purpose communication protocol, but facilitates specific use cases (called profiles) like audio streaming, network connections, file transfers, remote control, tracking, input/output devices, etc.

Types of Wireless

WLAN (Wireless LAN)

Technologies integrating wireless communication into well-known LANs, from the space of a room to an entire campus of buildings.

  • Wi-Fi (Wireless Fidelity) is the technology we use for bringing wired and wireless networking together in LANs. It’s standardized by IEEE 802.11 revisions (a/b/g/n/ac/ad).
  • Speeds went from 11 Mb/s in 1998 from the 1.5 Gb/s currently possible for ac devices. Ranges are up to 300m.
  • This is the kind of wireless network we we'll focus in this chapter.

WWAN (Wireless Wide-Area Network)

The wireless technologies enabled for long-distance communications across macro-areas, in the range of Kms. They are mainly used for providing Internet access.

  • Cellular is the ubiquitous wireless broadband. Every phone is now able to connect to the Internet with increasing speeds starting from 2G protocols (GSM, EDGE), to 3G devices (UMTS, HSPA+) and up to 4G (LTE, WiMAX).
  • WiMAX (IEEE 802.16) is wireless specifically tailored to provide broadband access. It has ranges up to 50 Km and speeds in the gigabit range.
  • Satellite broadband has been around for years, but only for receiving. It's now possible in TX/RX. It still is the only real option for broadband access in very remote areas.

Frequencies

Wireless communication technologies operate in the part of the electomagnetic spectrum known as radio waves, more precisely in the microwave range.

Frequencies: Low vs High

Lower frequencies radio waves have much better permeability, they penetrate dense objects such as buildings, while the higher the frequency the more a clear line of sight becomes a requirement.

Frequencies: Bands

The International Telecommunication Union - Radiocommunication Sector (ITU-R) handles the partition of the spectrum at the global level, which is then (sometimes heavily) regulated by governements in terms of allowed use and technical aspects.

Different wireless standards operate in different range of frequencies (called bands). WiFi uses unlicensed portions of the 2.4 GHz ISM (Industrial, Scientific and Medical) band and the 5 GHz UNII (Unlicensed National Information Infrastructure) band.

Frequencies: Channels

The frequency ranges in which 802.11 standards operate are subdivided into smaller ranges called channels. Channels are actually the combination of a central frequency and a bandwidth.

IEEE 802.11

The Working Group 11 of the IEEE 802 committee is in charge for a series of standards (revisions) about half-duplex wireless modulation techniques (L1, physical layer) and the frame format (L2, MAC layer) we commonly know as WiFi.

It also works on a series of amendments and extensions that later are incorporated into an official revision.

802.11-1997

  • First version of the WiFi standard, basically non-existent as of today.
  • Also known as legacy mode.
  • Operating in the 2.4 GHz ISM band with a 22 MHz bandwidth.
  • Supported data rates of 1 or 2 Mb/s.
  • Used FHSS and DSSS as modulation techniques.
  • Single antenna.
  • Never really adopted.

802.11a and 802.11b

  • Released in 1999, they were immensely successful and popularized WiFi.
  • 802.11b operates in the 2.4 GHz ISM band, 802.11a in the 5 GHz U-NII bands.
  • 20 MHz bandwidth for 802.11a, 22 MHz for 802.11b.
  • Rates are 6-54 Mb/s for 802.11a, 2-11 Mb/s for 802.11b.
  • 802.11a use OFDM modulation; 802.11b uses DSSS.
  • Single antenna.
  • Because of the different bands in use, the 2 standards are not compatible.
  • Thanks to its lower operating frequency, 802.11b has a better range than 802.11a.

802.11g

  • Released in 2003, updated with amendments in 2007.
  • Backwards-compatible with 802.11b
    • Same bandwidth (20 MHz) and frequencies (2.4 GHz band), and it also supports the DSSS modulation.
    • When 802.11g and b devices operate in a mixed setups, overall it will reduce the performance of the g devices.
    • This is true for any mixed-environments, even when involving more recents standards.
  • 802.11g use the same OFDM modulation introduced by 802.11a and so can bring its performance in the 2.4 GHz band: 6-54 Mb/s.
  • Single antenna.

802.11n

  • Released in 2009, updated with amendments in 2012.
  • Backwards-compatible with all previous standards (a/b/g), with obviously reduced performance when operating in mixed-setups.
  • Operating in both (dual band) 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands. However most n-devices operate in one or the other.
  • 802.11n allows to use 20 MHz bandwidth, but also supports 40 MHz bandwidth that doubles the theoretical throughput.
  • 802.11n introduces a technology called MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output) which use separated multiple antennas for simultaneous TX and RX.

802.11n: MIMO

MIMO also organize a single signal into multiple spatial streams. 802.11n supports up to 4 MIMO spatial streams.

802.11n devices reports their MIMO capabilities in a TX antennas x RX antennas : spatial streams format.

Ex.: a 3 x 3 : 2 MIMO configuration means 3 transmitting and receiving antennas, with 2 spatial streams supported.

While the data rates for a single (SISO) 20 MHz stream only went up from 54 to 65 Mb/s compared to 802.11g, when combined with the 40 MHz bandwidth and the multiple streams of MIMO, the maximum data rates can reach 600 Mb/s.

802.11n: MCS

The different combinations of all possible OFDM modulation parameters, bandwidth and spatial streams are know as numbered (1-31) MCS (Modulation Coding Schemes), each correlating to a maximum achievable data rate.

802.11ac

  • Released in 2013. Only operating in the 5 GHz band.
  • Compatible with 5 GHz 802.11n and 802.11a devices.
  • 20, 40 and now 80 and 160 MHz bandwidth are possible.
  • MIMO can be done with up to 8 spatial streams.
  • 802.11ac introduces Multiple User MIMO (MU-MIMO), which means the participating antennas don’t necessarily belong to the same device: you can use 2 wifi NICs to connect to the same AP and combine throughput.
  • With modulation improvements this means per-stream maximum data rate reached 86.7 Mb/s @ 20 MHz.
    • 2x2:2 MIMO can reach 867 Mb/s @ 80 MHz and 1.6 Gb/s @ 160 MHz.
    • Maximum rate for 8-streams MU-MIMO is 6.76 Gb/s.

802.11ad

  • Released in 2013, commercially known as WiGig.
  • Adds a 60 GHz operating band (still unlicensed), with a 2.1 GHz bandwidth.
  • It’s backwards compatible with previous 802.11 standards.
  • 60 GHz cannot penetrate through obstacles at all, so it is reserved for short distances or line-of-site communications.
  • However the protocols allows roaming, and the lower frequencies of the 2.4 and 5 GHz bands will be used.
  • Data rates can reach 7 Gb/s on the 60 GHz band.

WiFi Certifications

Vendors have the habits to introduce wireless standards way before their final ratification, under the form of drafts.

Draft implementations can be incompatible with each other and with the final standard, but this is less likely since the Wi-Fi Alliance, an association of vendors that certifies products for interoperability and promotes new capabilities.

WiFi Certifications

  • 802.11a/b/g/n/ac standards.
  • WPA2 and EAP security protocols.
  • WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup) - A feature to securely and easily connect devices without sharing the password.
  • Wi-Fi Direct - Using WPS, connects two devices directly (much like bluetooth pairing) for the purpose of sharing media much more easily.
  • Wi-Fi Passpoint - Securely connect to enabled hotspot with the capability for roaming.
  • Wi-Fi Miracast - Wi-Fi streaming of video and audio between devices.
  • Wireless Multimedia Extensions (WMM) - Basic QoS for Wi-Fi.

LAN vs WLAN

  • Radio waves are not contained like signals in a shielded cable. Anybody can receive them within their range and they are subject to interference.
  • Wireless signals travel as long as they can, losing strength along the way, while cables are optimized to maintain predictable signal strength over a specified distance.
  • Wireless compliance is not just about technical standards. The use of RF is subject to regulation. Specific bands could be unlicensed, limited, unavailable, depending on the country.

LAN vs WLAN

  • Instead of connecting through a switch, wireless devices participate in a WLAN by connecting to an Access Point (AP).
  • Enabling mobility as it does, Wireless is often used on battery-powered devices, challenging their duration.
  • 802.11 standards use CSMA/CA instead of collision detection, in order to actively avoid collision among competing hosts.
  • The L2 frame format is similar to Ethernet, but slightly different with additional informations.

Wireless Equipment

Contrary to popular beliefs, both ends of a wireless communication transmit and receive. There is however a meanigful classification between

  • End devices (referred to as STA, Stations)
  • Infrastructure devices, coordinating and collecting connections from end devices

End devices have Wireless NICs, operationally similar to the Ethernet NICs. They need proper driver installed to function.

Nowadays, basically every device integrates a wireless NICs. If not, USB Wireless adapters are popular and cheap.

Infrastructure Devices

The infrastructure device needed to provide wireless access in a LAN is called Access Point (AP), which bridges wireless and wired connectivity.

More often the AP functionality is found in a ISR (Integrated Service Router), which also works as a switch (bridging a wired LAN) and router (Internet gateway for the WLAN).

Home routers are almost always ISRs.

Infrastructure Devices

Powerlines are auxiliary infrastructure devices that extends an home WLAN outside the range of the AP, using power outlets as end points and electical wirings for data transfer.

In business and enterprise a stand-alone, more powerful AP is often needed to support the requirements. In such cases the AP is connected to a switch with a wired connection.

AP: Autonomous vs Controller-Based

APs can be either autonomous (configuration is done and stored on the device itself) or controller-based (configuration is automated and centralized on a server, or WLAN controller).

If only a few APs are needed, autonomous APs are a better fit. As the number of APs grow the initial configuration and maintainance is time-consuming. Controller-based APs can be just installed and the controller will auto-configure them.

AP: Autonomous vs Controller-Based

Some autonomous Cisco APs also support clustering which is the logical grouping of APs located in the same network segment and that can then be centrally controlled in software, without the need for a dedicated hardware controller.

For APs in the cluster a single configuration can be exported to all APs and there is channel and security management. An AP of the cluster becomes a Single Point Setup (SPS).

Enterprise WiFi Infrastructure

For large enterprise WLANs Cisco provides two controller-based architectures: the Cisco Meraki Cloud Managed and the Cisco Unified Wireless Network.

In the cloud architecture, the MR Cloud Managed APs (e.g.: MR12, MR16 or MR24) are configured and monitored from a web service (the MCC, Meraki Cloud Controller) acting as a replacement for a physical WLAN controller.

If the Internet access is disrupted, Meraki APs still are perfectly operational, they simply cannot be managed from the web dashboard. Only control traffic goes to and from the Meraki APs, not the actual datas belonging to the client.

Enterprise WiFi Infrastructure

In the unified architecture, the lightweight APs (like the Cisco Aironet series) only have the minimal functionality required to process wireless communications, while all management is done by a Wireless Controller (WLC) through the Lightweight Access Control Point Protocol (LWAPP).

Cisco WLCs are available in different forms: a stand-alone appliance, a dedicated virtual server running over VMWare, or as additional modules.

Antennas

Many APs have replaceable antennas to suite a range of possible conditions. Antennas’ most important features are their gain, beamwidth and radiation pattern.

Antennas

  • Omnidirectional - Stick-like looking with a 360° coverage which makes them ideal to deploy in the center of an area (open spaces, rooms). Almost every internal antenna is "omni".
  • Directional - They focus the signal emission and reception in the pointing direction and thereabouts, at the expense of the signal strength in other directions. Directional antennas are dishes and grids and have beamwidths of 40° or less.

Antennas

  • Sectorial - It's a term to define antennas having beamwidth around 100° or more, therefore able to cover a “sector”. They usually present themselves with a panel form.
  • Yagi - very directional antennas and thus able to travel longer distances with their higher gains. TV Antennas are Yagis.

802.11 APs mount 2+ antennas to take advantage of MIMO. More antennas typically mean better achievable MIMO modes.

Ad-Hoc Wireless Topology

Ad-Hoc Topology

In ad-hoc wireless, devices connect one another, without the need of an orchestrating device such as an AP.

Every device in an ad-hoc network has equal (p2p) status.

Only requirements to join an ad-hoc topology is to support this topology (called Indipendent Basic Service Set, IBSS, in 802.11 standards) and being in the coverage range of any other participating device.

Basically ad-hoc means that devices can spontaneously form a WLAN, without relying on existing infrastructure.

Examples of ad-hoc networks are bluetooth pairings, but mind that 802.11 ad-hoc is not limited to 2 devices.

Infrastructure Mode Topology

In an infrastructure mode topology, wireless devices play roles at different levels: APs and STA (stations).

There are two basic building blocks: the Basic Service Set (BSS) and the Extended Service Set (ESS).

BSS - Basic Service Set

BSS is the simplest infrastructure topology, because it consists of a single AP and its connected STAs.

The coverage area of the AP is called Basic Service Area (BSA). Outside of the BSA, a client cannot communicate with other devices in the BSS.

Therefore, BSS and BSA are often used as synonims.

Each BSS is uniquely identified by the L2 MAC address of its AP, which is known as Basic Service Set Identifier (BSSID). There is a 1:1 relationship between BSS and BSSID.

ESS - Extended Service Set

More than one BSS interconnected through a Distribution System (DS), thus creating an Extended Service Area (ESA) in which the BSS coverages can be overlapping and/or distinct.

ESS - Extended Service Set

  • STAs in a BSS can reach STAs in another BSS belonging to the same ESS. Traffic will flow through the DS.
  • A device can roam, that is move to a different BSS in the same ESS and automatically reconnect. This is possible because the ESS is identified by its SSID (Service Set Identifier), which we better know as name of the wireless network.

Tethering

Tethering is the practice of using a phone’s wireless radio as an AP in order to allow other devices to connect and use the Internet connection the mobile has over cellular broadband.

Basically, it is a BSS connected to a wireless distribution system.

However, Cisco categorizes tethering/mobile hotspots as ad-hoc networks...

802.11 Frame Format

802.11 Frame Format

  • Frame Control - Identifies the specific type of wireless frame and contains several subfields.
  • Duration - Mostly it indicates the remaining time to receive the next frame transmission.
  • Address 1, 2, 3, 4 - MAC addresses of the devices involved in the wireless communication. Which address goes into which fields depends on parameters in the frame control.
    • Addr. 1: always the receiving wireless device.
    • Addr. 2: always the transmitting wireless device.
    • Addr. 3: the actual destination device, maybe in the DS.
  • Sequence Control - Composed of two subfields, the sequence and fragment numbers, which respectively identifies a frame and its fragments.

802.11 Frame Format - Frame Control

  • Protocol Version - The particular 802.11 revision in use.
  • Frame Type and Subtype - 2 fields identifying the precise function of the frame, first telling if the frame is a control, data or management frame and then which associated subtype.
  • ToDS/FromDS - 2 bits that indicates if the frame is entering or exiting the Distribution System, respectively. Both are set to 0 if in ad-hoc mode (no infrastructure, hence no DS).
  • More Fragments - Has this frame been fragmented?
  • Retry - Indicates if this frame is a retransmission.

802.11 Frame Format - Frame Control

  • Power Management - Indicates if the device transmitting this frame is in power-saving mode or not. APs will never set this.
  • More Data - Signals to a device in power-saving mode that the AP has other data to send.
  • Security - Are encryption/authentication in use for the frame?
  • Reserved - Can be used to signal that all frames must be processed in the order they arrive.

Types of 802.11 Frames

  • 0x0, Management Frame - Its purpose is to establish and then maintain the communication, by discovering, authenticating and associating with an AP.
  • 0x1, Control Frame - Supporting function to aid communication, for instance by acknowledging and avoiding collisions.
  • 0x2, Data Frame - The frames that actually carry the payload.

802.11 Management Frames

  • 0x00, Association Request - It is sent from a STA and contains the SSID the STA wants to associate with and capabilities informations, like supported data rates.
  • 0x01, Association Response - Sent from AP to STA to signal if an association request has been accepted. If so, the AP generates an association ID (AID) for the STA and sends it along with supported data rates.
  • 0x02, Reassociation Request - Sent by a STA either while reconnecting after a short disconnection or if reconnecting to a stronger signal AP. The old and new APs coordinates exchange of informations about the STA.
  • 0x03, Reassociation Response - Same as an association response, but by a different AP.

802.11 Management Frames

  • 0x04, Probe Request - Used when a STA is actively scanning for information about APs.
  • 0x05, Probe Response - AP response to a STA probe, containing capabilities informations.
  • 0x08, Beacon - Used by the APs to periodically advertise the SSID/connection capabilites through the BSS
  • 0x0A, Disassociation - After association, either side can terminate the connection using this frame. STA are then removed from AP’s association table.
  • 0x0B, Authentication - STA send a frame containing their identity to the AP.
  • 0x0C, Deauthentication - Sent when all communication is terminated.

CSMA/CA: Collision Avoidance

Wireless is inherently half-duplex, which means that a radio cannot transmit and receive at the same time.

This would be a problem if 802.11 were to use the same CSMA/CD media contention method of Ethernet, because the radio wouldn’t sense a collision while transmitting. This is why a collision avoidance scheme was developed.

802.11 Control Frames are used to perform the CSMA/CA process, which resembles an handshake.

CSMA/CA: Collision Avoidance

  • 0x1B, RTS (Request to Send) - If it senses the medium around it as free, the STA sends this control frame to "book" the wireless medium for sending data frames.
  • 0x1C, CTS (Clear to Send) - AP response to a RTS frame, clearing the STA for transmission (if the medium is free). It includes a time value, so the other STAs sensing this frame will avoid transmitting during at least that timeframe.
  • 0x1D, ACK (Acknowledge) - Sent to acknowledge a data frame received with no errors to the sending device. If an ACK is not received in a certain interval (during which other STAs won’t transmit), the frame must be resent.

802.11 Process

It's simple:

  • Discover a WLAN
  • Authenticate with the AP
  • Associate with the AP

For this process to occur, the AP and the client must negotiate or agree to certain parameters.

802.11 Process

  • SSID - The name of the wireless network, 2-32 characters long. It will appear on the client as an available network connection, and can be shared between multiple APs in a ESS.
  • Password - The security key that client must know to pass the authentication stage and being able to associate to the AP. Only relevant is Security is enabled.
  • Security - The type of authentication+encryption mechanism in use. WEP, WPA and WPA2 are the most common options.

802.11 Process

  • Network Mode - The 802.11 revision the WLAN will operate in. Most of the time a Mixed mode will be selected, to support multiple standards at once.
  • Channel - Selection of the frequency of operation. There’s an automatic mode that allows the AP to select the channel with less interference.

WLAN Discovery

  • In passive mode, the AP signal its presence by the periodic transmission of a Beacon management frame, which advertise the SSID and the association parameters.
  • In active mode, the STAs send, on every channel iteratively, a Probe Request management frame (as unicast if the SSID is known, broadcast otherwise) containing supported network modes and data rates.

The AP can be configured to hide its SSID as a form of additional security, by disabling Beacon frames transmission.

A STA can connect to an hidden SSID only if it knows the SSID in advance in order to send a directed probe request. The hidden AP will "reveal" itself through a probe response.

WLAN Authentication

When a WLAN has no authentication required (open networks) the process reduces to a simple request and response. The authentication methods available for 802.11 relies on a shared key, pre-configured on both STA and AP.

  • STA sends authentication frame to AP.
  • AP responds with a challenge text.
  • STA encrypts the challenge text with its shared key and sends it to the AP.
  • The AP decrypts it with its shared keys.
  • If the decrypted text matches the original challenge text, it means that the STA had the same shared key of the APs so the authentication succeed. If they differ, it doesn't.

WLAN Association

  • Association request sent by the STA, in order to obtain an AID. It includes the STA MAC Address.
  • Association response from the AP, containing its BSSID (which is its MAC) and the generated AID. The AID works equivalently to a switch port.

Frequency/Channel Management

The technique used to represent data over the chosen frequency is called modulation.

Over the development of 802.11 these techniques improved to mitigate channel saturation by using it more efficiently.

  • DSSS, Direct-Sequence Spread Spectrum - Uses the entire frequency band to transmit the signal.

Frequency/Channel Management

  • FHSS, Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum - The band is divided into a series of subchannels, each used as the carrier only for a short period, and then both the AP and the STA switch to another one based on a pseudorandom sequence.
  • OFDM, Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing - In the operating band, a set of subcarrier frequencies are chosen . The signal is then demultiplexed over these subcarriers and multiplexed at the receiver.

Channel Selection

802.11 2.4 GHz band is subdivided into several channels (11 for the US, 13 in Europe, 14 in other regions).

These channels have central frequencies 5 MHz apart.

Of course with a 20/22 MHz bandwidth this means that adjacent channels will partially overlap.

Channel Selection

Interference is inevitable with overlapping channels.

Notice however how a subset of channels, precisely those 5 channel apart, are non-overlapping. The maximum number of non-overlapping channels is 3 (Ch. 1, 6, 11).

Using non-overlapping channels in a multi-AP environment is the best choice to avoid interference.

Channel Selection

Newer 802.11 standards supports 40 MHz-channels. With such a large bandwidth, it’s impossible to have non-overlapping configurations in the 2.4 GHz band, and non-overlapping channels in the 5 GHz bands are reduced.

WLAN Deployment

The performances and coverage are of a WLAN depends on several factors:

  • Characteristics of the facility
  • Environmental factors
  • Number of users
  • Supported standards and data rates
  • Antenna and their patterns
  • Transmit power

WLAN Deployment

Determining the extent of the coverage area requires to analyze the AP and antenna specifications and to perform a site survey, maybe with the aid of a spectrum analyzer.

By slightly overlapping (10-15%) single BSAs in an ESS configured with the same SSID, we enable roaming.

WLAN Attacks: DoS

WLAN introduces a different threat model for network security, because the attacker may not need physical access to directly attack the local network.

Wireless connectivity can be denied (DoS) by a misconfigured device or by interference, either accidental (even by non-computing devices operating in the same band) or malicious (frequency jamming).

Both can be fought by continuosly monitoring the spectrum. In this way the problem can be solved as soon as it happens.

WLAN Attacks: Protocol

DoS attacks can also be carried out against the 802.11 protocol by manipulating management and control frames. For instance:

  • Spoofed disassociation requests, where the attacker disassociates on behalf of all STAs, that when reassociating simultaneously cause a spike in traffic. 802.11i extensions can help protect again spoofing.
  • CTS flood, where the attacker sends multiple CTS frames from a STA not actually associated to the BSS. This will cause legitimate STAs in range to whithold transmission. 802.11w extension deal with protecting management frames from manipulation.

WLAN Attacks: Rogue AP

Rogue APs are unauthorized Access Points. Whether they are installed by an attacker of by a well-meaning individual, the effect is to allow unauthorized devices in the DS, and completely bypass the deployed security measures.

Rogue APs are used to carry MITM attacks. A Rogue APs with the same SSID, but with a stronger signal or no authentication, would be preferred by STAs over legitimate ones.

WLAN Attacks: Rogue AP

The STAs could be unsuspecting, because there’s no obvious clue and also most STAs are configured to automatically connect to open networks. Rogue APs are configured to forward the data back and forth to the legitimate AP, after interception.

Detecting Rogue APs is difficult, it requires constant monitoring of the WLAN (with IPS and RRM, Radio Resource Management systems) and authentication of all its users.

WLAN Security: Hidden SSID

A first step in protecting a WLAN could be to hide the SSID and to use ACLs to allow only authorized MAC addresses.

However these are not reliable, because both these informations are sent in clear text over the air and so easily spoofable.

802.11 Authentication Methods

The 3 authentication methods available for 802.11 are:

  • WEP, Wireless Equivalent Privacy - Uses the RC4 encryption algorithm with a static key that never changes between frames, makes it easier to crack. It’s been broken since 2001. Long story short: never use it.
  • WPA, WiFi Protected Access - Essentially WEP with a different encryption algo, TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol) which uses a different key for each frame.
  • WPA2 (IEEE 802.11i) - Uses the most advanced encryption algorithm available, which is AES (Advanced Encryption Standard). This is the current best practice to deploy to secure WiFi authentication.

Enterprise WLAN Security

Pre-shared keys are simple but have a major flaw: the credential being the same for everyone. In enterprise networks, it's best to have a dedicated AAA (Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting) server, like RADIUS.

In such setups, the AP forwards authentication request to and from a RADIUS server (which only the AP is allowed to contact). Clients will authenticate using the 802.1X standard and EAP (Extensible Authentication Protocol).

User credentials are stored only on the RADIUS server, not the AP. The AP only needs a pre-shared key (and the IP/ports) to authenticate itself to the RADIUS server.
