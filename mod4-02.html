WANs - Wide Area Networks

WANs are computer networks just as LANs, but spanning over an extended area, connecting geographically distant nodes (often entire LANs).

While LANs are under the administration and are property of an organization, WANs are usually operated by service providers (often telephone, cable, satellite companies) rented for a fee.

They are needed when an organization wants to connect several LANs (campus) under its control in disperse locations (branches) without the immense cost involved in creating long range high-speed links.

WANs - Wide Area Networks

Internet is a cheaper alternative to private/leased WANs. In this case the organization will connect its remote locations using its internet connection and the data will travel over the public internet (which is why it must be encrypted).

The wide-area needs of an organization are often first addressed with the Internet, moving to dedicated WAN technologies only when economically justifiable.

WAN Standards

WAN standards operate at the

  • Physical Layer (L1), describing electrical, physical, mechanical characteristics of the WAN technology in exam.
  • Data Link Layer (L2), describing how data are organized (framed, encapsulated) before being sent over the wire and the mechanisms to coordinate the transmission.
    • WAN links are generally PtP, so addressing might not be needed.

WAN Slang

WAN Slang

  • Customer Premises Equipment (CPE) - Devices that are located at the subscriber (a purchaser of WAN services from a TLC operator) for the purpose of accessing the service. They can be either owned or leased.
    • Data Terminal Equipment (DTE) - The device collecting data from the subsciber network to be put on the WAN link. It connects to the WAN link indirectly, through a DCE.
    • Data Communications Equipment (DCE, Data Circuit-Terminating Equipment) - The device that actually puts/gets data to/from the local loop.

WAN Slang

  • Demarcation Point - The point separating CPEs from devices belonging to the provider. Here the responsibility for the connection changes.
  • Local Loop - The wire connecting the CPE to the provider’s central. Often called the last mile.
  • Central Office - A structure belonging to the provider where the local loop is connected to its long-distance, digital infrastructure (Toll Network).

WAN Devices

WAN Devices

  • Not an exact list, depends on the WAN tech in use.
  • (Dialup/Broadband) Modem - Modems are devices that encode/decode digital information in a form suitable for transmission. Dialup modems use (modulate) voice frequencies over telephone lines to encode data.
    • Broadband modems use higher, better frequencies to achieve higher speed over telephone or cable lines.
  • Access Server - A device with multiple interfaces aggregating and serving the “calls” made by multiple modems. i.e., a DSL provider can have access servers to authenticate customers through PPPoE.
  • CSU/DSU - A modem-like device to interface with a WAN link over digital leased lines. Channel Service Units and Data Service Units are often integrated in a single device.

WAN Devices

  • WAN Switch - They switch traffic at L2 in the provider’s network.
  • Router - Device with multiple interfaces internetworking the LAN and the WAN link. WAN interfaces connects to the service provide either directly or mediated by some external device (modem or DSU/CSU).
  • Core Router/Multilayer Switch - the highest-speed forwarding devices in the provider’s network, sitting right in the middle of it.

Circuit Switching

Circuit Switching

A communication in which a dedicated path between two devices has to be established through the provider network before any data can be trasmitted.

Any two endpoints request a connection to the network, which responds by setting up a virtually dedicated path (network becomes transparent) between them.

  • The oldest networking technology, 100+ years old.
  • Extremely used, telephone network is circuit-switched communication
  • Inefficient, 100% dedicated but rarely 100% utilized.
    • As long as the communication is established the path cannot be used by other parties, even if idle/underutilized.

Packet Switching

Packet Switching

In packet-switching, data are splitted into blocks (packets) and the network determine the path on a per-packet basis, allowing

  • multiple nodes to communicate over the same path.
  • multiple paths to be used for the same communication.

To be able to dynamically select a path (routing), the network requires addressing information in each packet. There are 2 approaches:

  • Connectionless - Full addressing in each packet, the route is evaluated for each one by looking at these informations. This is how IP works.

Packet Switching

  • Connection-oriented - The network predetermines the route for a set of packets; packets carry an identifier (label). Then, that route (Virtual Circuit) is associated with that identifier, and subsequent routing decisions follow this association.
    • Similar in principle to circuit-switcrhing, but paths or part of them can still be shared. Packets are not routed individually.
    • When the "circuit" is set up on-demand, and breaks after the transmission has ended, it’s a Switched Virtual Circuit (SVC).
    • A VC that is available by network configuration and does not need call setup is a Permanent Virtual Circuit (PVC).

WAN Infrastructures

  • Private WAN - Specifically purchased connection option for WAN purposes from a service provider, either a dedicated PtP leased line, circuit-switched or packet-switched links.
  • Public WAN - Using the public Internet as a WAN connection. Private data must be secured while on transit over a public network.

WAN Infrastructures

WAN infrastructure from the service provider side it’s made up by high-speed fiber opticer optic.

The network standard in use are Synchronous Optical Networking (SONET) and Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH). Basically the same standard, respectively American and European-based.

A new development for long-haul connections is DWDM, Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing.

  • Bidirectionality and 80+ channels over a single fiber.
  • Signals extracted from specific wavelength.
  • 10 Gb/s per-channel.
  • Amplification of optical signals supported.

Private WAN: Leased Lines

Private WAN: Leased Lines

Leased lines are dedicated, PtP connections between sites belonging to the subscriber of the WAN service.

Subscriber’s networks at both ends are connected to the SP network through one of its PoP (Point of Presence).

They're called leased, because the subscriber pays a certain fee based on bandwidth and distance between connected sites.

Leased lines’ data rate is defined through the T-carrier system (T1/T3, 1.544 and 43.7 Mb/s respectively) in North America and the E-carrier (E1/E3, 2.048 and 34.368 Mb/s) system in Europe.

  • PtP links are simple to setup and maintain
  • Dedicated lines are exclusively used by the subscriber, removing latency and jitter issues, also improving availability.
  • Exclusive use costs way more than shared/public connections, performances being equal.

Private WAN: Dialup/PSTN

When leased line aren’t available, the ubiquity of copper telephone lines can be used for dedicated, circuit-switched WAN services, albeit at much slower speeds.

Telephone lines of the PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network) can be adapted for carrying binary data by using a modem.

Speeds however are limited by the physical features of the local loop (the copper wiring) to 56 Kb/s or less.

Dialup can still be the only option in some remote areas.

Private WAN: ISDN

Private WAN: ISDN

Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN) is a circuit-switching technology for an all-digital PSTN local loop, thus achieving faster speeds than dialup.

This is done by using time-division multiplexed (TDM) digital signals (in short, each signal has its own time slot). Each signal is a subchannel.

  • A bearer (B) channel is a 64 Kbps channel used for data and voice.
  • A delta (D) channel is a 16/64 Kbps channel used for signaling and setup.

Private WAN: ISDN

ISDN interfaces can be:

  • Basic Rate (BRI) - It provides 2x 64 Kbps B channels and 1x 16 Kbps D control channel, for a typical bandwidth of 128 Kbps, enough for home and SOHO scenarios.
  • Primary Rate (PRI) - A larger ISDN installation with
    • 23x B @ 64 Kbps + 1x D @ 64 Kbps = 1.544 Mbps (North America)
    • 30x B @ 64 Kbps + 1x D @ 64 Hbps = 2.048 Mbps (Europe/Australia)

ISDN may require a special adapter to be added to the router, called terminal adapter (TA), to be able to setup a connection.

Private WAN: Frame Relay

Private WAN: Frame Relay

Frame Relay is a L2 NBMA (non-broadcast, multiaccess) protocol for WAN aimed to provide cheaper alternative to leased lines.

A router with a frame relay interface (DTE) plugs (typically with twisted copper or fiber) at the edge of provider’s frame relay network, made of several frame relay switches (DCE).

Frame relay then connects each site with a PtP connection that runs over a virtual circuit (VC) established over the frame relay packet-switched network.

VCs on a single leased line are identified using data-link connection identifiers (DLCIs), ensuring bidirectional transmission between specific DTEs.

Private WAN: ATM

Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM) is a technology for network backbone designed to be able to transfer both high-throughput data and low-latency transmissions. It maps roughly to the L1, L2, L3 layers of the ISO/OSI stack.

Its main peculiarity is that it doesn’t use variable-size frames, but instead it’s built on small fixed-size cells (53 bytes, 48 payload and 5 header).

This minimize queuing issue present in IP for instance, when small packets have to wait larger content transfers during the forwarding process.

Private WAN: ATM

Having small fixed-size blocks allows to more easily perform the network processing in hardware.

The downside it’s less efficiency (~20%), because of the higher overhead in the cell and in the reassembly process.

When ATM was designed, queuing was a serious problem in slower links (7.8 ms to transfer a 1500 bytes IP packet on T1). Today, faster links (10 Gbps = 3 µs) lessen the need for small cells in the network backbone.

ATM can scale easily to faster speeds and uses VCs, both PVCs and SVCs.

Private WAN: Ethernet

Ethernet was born to be a LAN technology (high speed but low distances).

Recent developments using fiber optics (such as standards IEEE 1000BASE-LX or IEEE1000BASE-ZX, up to 70 km) allowed Ethernet to be used as a WAN technology.

Ethernet WAN is also known as:

  • Metropolitan Ethernet, MetroEthernet
  • Etherner over MPLS (EoMPLS)
  • Virtual Private LAN Service (VPLS)

The main advantages are:

  • Very easy to integrate in existing LANs, often no need for additional hardware.
  • First-class support for IP applications.

Private WAN: MPLS

Private WAN: MPLS

Multiprotocol Label Switching is a network backbone technology that can be considered as a next-gen Frame Relay/ATM.

Its main features is that it routes packet based solely on a label applied to it. The label identifies paths, not the endpoints of the communication (as DLCIs do in Frame Relay, for instance).

This allows to create end-to-end, PtP connections between endpoints regardless of the protocol. Frame Relay, ATM, IPv4/6, DSL, can all be encapsulated in MPLS, that is L2-independent.

Label are added and removed at the provider edge routers, which interfaces with the customer edge in a specific access technology. Labels are then processed by the SP internal MPLS network.

Private WAN: VSAT

Very Small Aperture Terminal (VSAT) is a WAN technology that uses satellites. It comes in handy when there are no copper/fiber options.

It uses dishes similar to those bringing satellite TV in homes, but they are instead connected to a router.

The connection takes place thanks to a geosynchronous orbit satellite belonging to the service provider.

The endpoint dishes are pointed towards it and the transmissions bounced back and forth to Earth, travelling more than 35000 Kms.

Public WAN: DSL, Cable, Cellular

DSL (Digital Subscriber Line) and Cable use existing telephone and cable tv lines to provide an always-on connection to the provider network.

This leverages a public WAN infrastructure, because multiple subscribers share the infrastructure connecting them to the WAN provider.

  • In DSL, the various last miles are aggregated into a single high capacity link at the provider’s PoP, using a DSLAM (DSL Access Multiplexer).
  • In Cable, even the access cable for local subscribers is shared, thus reducing the bandwidth when new users join.

Public WAN: DSL, Cable, Cellular

In both cases, a WAN connection between sites is achieved through the public Internet and not directly through the provider’s network, so the WAN traffic will flow across multiple 3rd-parties.

There are many DSL standards (ADSL, HDSL, VDSL, etc), differing mostly for coding and modulation techniques.

All of the above applies to mobile broadband: it uses the existing cellular network shared with all the subscribers, establishing data connections through different standards (3G = UMTS/HSDPA, 4G = LTE/WiMAX)

Public WAN: VPN

Virtual Private Network (VPN) technology is essential when using a public infrastructure to obtain WAN services.

This is because data will, at some point, flow through the public internet. if not encrypted, any 3rd party would be able to read them.

VPNs established a Layer 2 encrypted connection, called tunnel.

  • The tunnel is routed through the public Internet, but still appear as a direct connection at the data link layer to the endpoints.
  • This is because the VPN datagram
    • is encapsulated over TCP/UDP (above the L4)
    • have its payload containing new headers for the L2 and above.

Public WAN: VPN

The VPN tunnel is encrypted. One it reaches the other endpoint, it is decrypted and the plaintext payload is passed to the network stack at L2, transparently. VPN access can be established as a:

  • Site-to-Site VPN - Connecting entire networks. VPN software is configured on a central device (router, firewall, security appliance, etc).
  • Remote-Access VPN - Connecting individual hosts, running VPN client on their end devices (phones, laptop, PC, tablet, etc).

Choosing a WAN Service

  • Purpose
    • type of users to be connected (internal, partner, customer, ?)
    • Limited or full access to which users?
  • Geographical Scope
    • Area: local, regional, global?
    • Branches: one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many?
  • Traffic Requirements
    • Type of traffic (data, voice, video, large/small files, real time?)
    • Traffic volume (determines bandwidth requirements)
    • QoS support

Choosing a WAN Service

  • Private or Public infrastrucure? Dedicated or Switched (if private)?
    • Depends where the balance between requirements and costs lies.
  • Site-to-Site or Remote-Access VPN? Or both?
  • Local availability
    • Not all WAN services are available everywhere, so some theoretically better option are not feasible.
  • Cost
    • Sometimes this is the first and only costraint, and everything else is chosen to provide the best option fitting this limitation.
